Beginner's Guide

Composting 101: Turning Waste Into Garden Gold

Discover how to transform kitchen scraps and yard waste into nutrient-rich soil that will make your plants thrive.

Start Composting Watch Tutorial

Every year, the average household throws away hundreds of pounds of organic waste that could be turned into "black gold" for your garden. Composting is nature's way of recycling, and with this guide, you'll learn how to harness this powerful process right in your backyard.

Did You Know?

Composting can divert up to 30% of household waste from landfills, where it would otherwise produce methane, a potent greenhouse gas.

Getting Started: The Basics

Composting requires just four basic ingredients in the right proportions:

Greens

Nitrogen-rich materials like vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, and fresh grass clippings.

Browns

Carbon-rich materials like dried leaves, straw, and shredded paper.

Water

Moisture to help break down organic matter.

Air

Oxygen for the microorganisms doing the work.

The Perfect Ratio

Aim for a 2:1 ratio of browns to greens by volume. This provides the ideal carbon-to-nitrogen balance for efficient decomposition.

Layering greens and browns creates the perfect composting environment

What You Can Compost

Do Compost

  • Fruit and vegetable scraps
  • Eggshells
  • Coffee grounds and filters
  • Tea bags (no staples)
  • Nut shells
  • Yard trimmings
  • Shredded newspaper

Don't Compost

  • Meat or fish scraps
  • Dairy products
  • Fats and oils
  • Pet wastes
  • Diseased plants
  • Coal or charcoal ash
  • Chemically treated wood

The Composting Timeline

Week 1-2: Initial Breakdown

Microorganisms begin breaking down soft materials. The pile will heat up as bacteria multiply.

Week 3-4: Active Phase

Temperature peaks (can reach 130-160°F). Turn the pile to incorporate oxygen.

Month 2-3: Cooling Down

Materials darken and break down further. Fungi and earthworms become more active.

Month 3-6: Finished Compost

The pile cools completely. Compost is dark, crumbly, and smells earthy.

Composting Benefits

Environmental

Reduces landfill waste and methane emissions while conserving resources.

Garden

Creates nutrient-rich soil that improves plant growth and water retention.

Economic

Saves money on fertilizers, soil amendments, and garbage disposal.

Troubleshooting Tips

Smelly compost? Add more browns and turn the pile to aerate.
Not decomposing? Add greens or moisten dry materials.
Attracting pests? Bury food scraps under brown layers.

Quick Start Guide

  1. Choose a shady spot near a water source
  2. Start with a layer of coarse browns
  3. Alternate 2 parts browns to 1 part greens
  4. Keep moist like a wrung-out sponge
  5. Turn pile every 2-4 weeks
  6. Harvest when dark and crumbly (3-6 months)
